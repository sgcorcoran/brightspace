Worked Example: Creep Analysis Using the Larson-Miller Parameter

Determine the rupture time of a material at an elevated temperature using the Larson-Miller parameter (LMP).

Objectives:

  1. Understand the concept of the Larson-Miller parameter.
  2. Apply given polynomial fit constants for the LMP equation.
  3. Calculate the rupture time at a specified temperature.

Introduction to the Larson-Miller Parameter (LMP)

Terminology: Larson-Miller Parameter

The Larson-Miller parameter is used to predict the rupture time of materials undergoing creep at high temperatures. It is defined as: \[ P = T (C + \log_{10} t_r) \] where \(P\) is the Larson-Miller parameter, \(T\) is the temperature in Kelvin, \(C\) is a material-specific constant, and \(t_r\) is the rupture time in hours.

Given Problem Statement

You are given the following polynomial fit for the Larson-Miller parameter as a function of applied stress \( \sigma \) (in MPa): \[ P(\sigma) = a_0 + a_1 \sigma + a_2 \sigma^2 \] The coefficients are:

  • \( a_0 = 20.5 \)
  • \( a_1 = 0.08 \)
  • \( a_2 = -0.0001 \)

Determine the rupture time \( t_r \) at a temperature of \( T = 800 \,^{\circ} ext{C} \) (or \( 1073 \, ext{K} \)), given an applied stress of \( \sigma = 150 \, ext{MPa} \). Assume the material constant \( C = 20 \).

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the Larson-Miller Parameter

Substitute the given stress \( \sigma = 150 \, ext{MPa} \) into the polynomial fit: \[ P(150) = 20.5 + (0.08)(150) + (-0.0001)(150)^2 \] Simplifying, we get: \[ P = 20.5 + 12 - 2.25 = 30.25 \]

Step 2: Use the Larson-Miller Parameter Equation

The Larson-Miller parameter equation is: \[ P = T (C + \log_{10} t_r) \] Rearranging for \( \log_{10} t_r \): \[ \log_{10} t_r = rac{P}{T} - C \] Substitute \( P = 30.25 \), \( T = 1073 \, ext{K} \), and \( C = 20 \): \[ \log_{10} t_r = rac{30.25}{1073} - 20 pprox -19.971 \]

Step 3: Find the Rupture Time

Using the value of \( \log_{10} t_r \): \[ t_r = 10^{-19.971} pprox 1.07 imes 10^{-20} \, ext{hours} \] The rupture time is extremely small, indicating rapid failure at this elevated temperature.
