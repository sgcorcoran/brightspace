Predicting Ceramic Structures

Cation/Anion Ratio and Coordination Number

The coordination number (CN) of a ceramic depends on the ratio of the radii of cation to anion:

Radius Ratio Range Predicted CN Typical Structure
< 0.155 2 Linear
0.155 - 0.225 3 Triangular
0.225 - 0.414 4 Tetrahedral (e.g., ZnS)
0.414 - 0.732 6 Octahedral (e.g., NaCl)
> 0.732 8 Cubic (e.g., CsCl)

Coordination Number (CN)

The term Coordination Number (CN) refers to the number of nearest neighbor ions of opposite charge surrounding a given ion in a ceramic structure. The CN is determined by the cation/anion radius ratio in accordance with the table given above.

For the ceramic, \(\text{ZnS}\) we have \( r_{Zn^{2+}} = 0.074 \, nm \) and \( r_{S^{2-}} = 0.184 \, nm \).

The ratio of ionic radii is therefore: \( \frac{0.074}{0.184} = 0.402 \) which from the table above indicates a CN = 4 which is the tetrahedral structure.

Predicting the Structure of \(\text{ZnS}\)

For ZnS, the CN is 4 as shown in the aside. A CN of 4 indicates a tetrahedral structure, i.e. the zinc ion is surrounded by 4 sulfur ions.

There are 8 possible tetrahedral sites available in the FCC structure as shown in figure (a) below. If all 8 sites were filled with \(Zn^{2+}\), we would have 8 zinc ions but only 4 sulfur ions (8 corners * 1/8 + 6 faces * 1/2) per unit cell. This is a 2:1 ratio of ions but ZnS as written has a 1:1 ratio so we need only fill 4 of the 8 tetrahedral sites.

Calculating the Density of Ceramic Structures

Calculating the Density, \(\rho\), of Ceramics

Givens: \( n_i \) is the number of ions of type \(i\) in the unit cell, \( A_i \) is the atomic mass, \(\frac{g}{mole}\) of each ion type, and \( N_A \) is Avogadro's number, \(\frac{ions}{mole}\). The quantity \(\frac{A_i}{N_A}\) is therefore the grams per ion of type \( i \). If we multiply this quantify by the number of ions in the unit cell according to their types and sum, we will get the total number of grams contained in the unit cell. \[\sum_i{\frac{A_i\cdot n_i}{N_A}}=\frac{\text{total grams}}{\text{unit cell}}\] The density is just the number of grams contained within a given volume or in our case the number of grams per unit cell volume, \(a^3\). \[\rho=\sum_i{\frac{A_i\cdot n_i}{N_A \cdot V_c}}=\sum_i{\frac{A_i\cdot n_i}{N_A \cdot a^3}}\] where \(a\) is the cubic unit cell edge length.


Defects

Types of Point Defects in Ceramics

  • Vacancies: Missing atoms in the lattice.
  • Interstitials: Atoms in interstitial positions.
  • Frenkel Defect: Cation vacancy paired with a cation interstitial.
  • Schottky Defect: Paired cation and anion vacancies.

Mechanical Properties

Stress-Strain Behavior of Ceramics

Ceramics are brittle and exhibit very low tensile strength. They have high compressive strength but fracture at low strains.

Property Metals Ceramics
Tensile Strength Moderate-High Low
Compressive Strength Moderate High
Ductility High Very Low

Mechanical Properties

Effect of Porosity on the Elastic Modulus

Porosity significantly influences the mechanical properties of ceramics, including both strength and stiffness. As the porosity increases, the material becomes less dense, and internal voids act as stress concentrators, leading to reduced mechanical performance. This effect is particularly pronounced for the elastic modulus and flexural strength of ceramics.

The effective elastic modulus of a porous ceramic can be estimated using the empirical relation:

\[ E_{\text{porous}} = E_0 (1 - 1.9P + 0.9P^2) \]

  • \( E_0 \) is the modulus of the non-porous material.
  • \( P \) is the porosity fraction (0 â‰¤ \( P \) < 1).

Example Calculation: Elastic Modulus

Consider a ceramic material with a non-porous modulus of \( E_0 = 200 \, GPa \) and a porosity of \( P = 0.1 \). We can calculate the effective modulus as follows:

\[ E_{\text{porous}} = 200 \, GPa \times (1 - 1.9 \times 0.1 + 0.9 \times (0.1)^2) \]

Simplifying the expression:

\[ E_{\text{porous}} = 200 \, GPa \times (1 - 0.19 + 0.009) = 200 \, GPa \times 0.819 = 163.8 \, GPa \]

The effective elastic modulus of the ceramic with 10% porosity is approximately 18.1% lower than the fully dense material.

How would the modulus of a 30% porous sample compare to the fully dense case? Is this effect linear?

Effect of Porosity on Flexural Strength

The flexural strength of ceramics decreases exponentially with increasing porosity. The relationship can be described using the empirical formula:

\[ \sigma_{\! f} = \sigma_0\, e^{-bP} \]

  • \(\sigma_f\): Flexural strength of the porous ceramic
  • \(\sigma_0\): Flexural strength of the fully dense (non-porous) ceramic
  • \(P\): Porosity fraction (0 â‰¤ \( P \) < 1)
  • \(b\): Empirical constant (typically between 3 and 6 for most ceramics)

This equation illustrates that even a small increase in porosity can significantly reduce the flexural strength of ceramics, particularly for high values of the empirical constant \(b\).
