Worked Example: Stress-Strain Curve Analysis

Interpret the stress-strain behavior of a metallic alloy (Steel) and a polymer (Polyurethane), and calculate key mechanical properties.

Objectives:

  1. Understand and interpret the key features of a stress-strain curve.
  2. Calculate mechanical properties such as yield strength, ultimate tensile strength (UTS), and fracture strain.

Key Concept: Stress-Strain Curve Interpretation

The stress-strain curve provides valuable information about a material’s mechanical properties. Key features include:

  • Yield Strength: The stress at which a material begins to deform plastically.
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS): The maximum stress a material can withstand before necking.
  • Fracture Strain: The strain at which the material breaks.

The shape of the curve varies significantly between ductile metals and viscoelastic polymers.

Expanded Problem Statement

Analyze the stress-strain data for:

  • A low-carbon steel sample with the following key points:
    • Yield stress: \( 250 \, \text{MPa} \)
    • Ultimate tensile strength: \( 450 \, \text{MPa} \)
    • Fracture at \( \epsilon_f = 0.3 \)
  • A polyurethane sample with:
    • Yield stress: \( 20 \, \text{MPa} \)
    • Ultimate tensile strength: \( 30 \, \text{MPa} \)
    • Fracture at \( \epsilon_f = 1.5 \)

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the Modulus of Elasticity (Young’s Modulus)

Metallic Material: Low-Carbon Steel

The modulus of elasticity can be approximated from the linear portion of the stress-strain curve:

\[ E = \frac{\sigma_{\text{yield}}}{\epsilon_{\text{yield}}} \]

Assume yield strain \( \epsilon_{\text{yield}} = 0.0012 \):

\[ E = \frac{250 \, \text{MPa}}{0.0012} = 208.3 \, \text{GPa} \]

Polymeric Material: Polyurethane

Assume yield strain \( \epsilon_{\text{yield}} = 0.02 \):

\[ E = \frac{20 \, \text{MPa}}{0.02} = 1.0 \, \text{GPa} \]

Step 2: Determine the Toughness (Area Under the Curve)

Steel

The toughness is the total area under the stress-strain curve, approximated by trapezoidal integration:

\[ \text{Toughness} \approx \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_{\text{yield}} + \sigma_{\text{UTS}}) \times \epsilon_f = \frac{1}{2}(250 + 450) \times 0.3 = 105 \, \text{MPa} \]

Polyurethane

\[ \text{Toughness} \approx \frac{1}{2}(20 + 30) \times 1.5 = 37.5 \, \text{MPa} \]

Step 3: Analyze Necking and Fracture Behavior

For the steel sample, necking occurs after reaching the UTS, followed by ductile fracture. For the polyurethane, the material shows a significant amount of elongation before breaking, indicative of its viscoelastic nature.

Discuss the differences in behavior between the steel and polyurethane samples.

Consider how the mechanical properties and curve shapes reflect the underlying material characteristics.
