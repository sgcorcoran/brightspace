Worked Example: Total Length of Dislocations in \(1\,\text{cm}^3\)

Estimate the total length of dislocations for both low and high dislocation density materials.

Objectives:

  1. Understand the concept of dislocation density.
  2. Calculate the total length of dislocations in a given volume of material.

Key Concept: Dislocation Density

Dislocation density (\( \rho \)) is defined as the total length of dislocations per unit volume. It is typically measured in units of \( \text{m/m}^3 \). High dislocation density corresponds to heavily deformed materials, while low dislocation density is characteristic of annealed materials.

\[ \text{Total Length} = \rho \times \text{Volume} \]

Expanded Problem Statement

Calculate the total length of dislocations in \(1 \, \text{cm}^3\) of Copper for:

  • Low dislocation density (\( \rho = 10^8 \, \text{m/m}^3 \)).
  • High dislocation density (\( \rho = 10^{12} \, \text{m/m}^3 \)).

Solution

Step 1: Convert Volume to SI Units

\( 1 \, \text{cm}^3 = 1 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m}^3 \)

Step 2: Calculate Total Length of Dislocations

For low dislocation density:

\[ \text{Total Length} = (10^8 \, \text{m/m}^3)(1 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m}^3) = 100 \, \text{m} \]

For high dislocation density:

\[ \text{Total Length} = (10^{12} \, \text{m/m}^3)(1 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m}^3) = 10^6 \, \text{m} \]
