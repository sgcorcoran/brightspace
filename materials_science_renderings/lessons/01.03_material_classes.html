Classification of Materials

Engineered Materials

Materials are everywhere, from natural materials such as wool, wood, or stone to engineered materials like steel, acrylic, glass, or concrete. Natural materials require little human modification to be put to use whereas engineered materials undergo significant processing. This course will focus on understanding the structure, properties, and processing of engineered materials.

The major classes of engineered materials are typically given as metals, ceramics, and polymers but from a practical point of view it may be better to expand this list to metals, ceramics, glasses, polymers, elastomers, and composites. In this course, we will use the expanded scheme.

Why do we need an expanded classification scheme beyond just metals, ceramics, and polymers? The expanded scheme is based on the similarities of materials with respect to their most basic properties and processing methods. Glasses for example could be included in the ceramics class but ceramics are mostly crystalline and processed by high temperature sintering whereas glasses are amorphous, transparent and processed while a viscous liquid.

In the context of this course, we will not include semiconductors in our expanded classification scheme. While doing so would not be incorrect, it could lead to the inclusion of other specialized categories such as biomedical materials, nanomaterials, quantum materials, or biomaterials, making the classification system overly complex.

Semiconductors themselves span a range of materials, including ceramic compounds like silicon carbide (SiC), gallium nitride (GaN), and titanium dioxide (TiO₂), as well as elemental semiconductors such as silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge). These materials play a crucial role in electronic and optoelectronic applications but fall outside the primary focus of this course.
