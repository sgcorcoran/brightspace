Bond Types in Materials

Bonding in Water

It is important to distinguish between the covalent bonds within a water molecule and the secondary bonding (hydrogen bonds) between water molecules. Missing this distinction can lead to misunderstandings. More than once, I have had students incorrectly recall from high school chemistry that covalent bonds are weak and water is used as the example to support this. Let's take a look at the intra- and inter- molecular bonds of water.

  • Covalent Bonds within Water Molecules: The two hydrogen atoms in a water molecule are bonded to the oxygen atom via strong covalent bonds. Each covalent bond has an energy of approximately 460 kJ/mol (~4.8 eV/atom), making it significantly stronger than any secondary bond.
  • Hydrogen Bonds between Water Molecules: Secondary bonding occurs when the partially positive hydrogen of one water molecule interacts with the partially negative oxygen of another forming a hydrogen bond. This bond is weak with a bond energy of approximately 20 kJ/mol (~0.2 eV/atom).

But water has a low melting point so the bonds are weak? True, but the bonds that are weak here are the bonds holding the water molecules to one another i.e the inter-molecular bonds (hydrogen bonding). The bonds within the water molecule (intra-molecular) are covalent and do not break until excessively high temperatures.

To illustrate further:

  • Boiling water (~100°C) breaks the hydrogen bonds between molecules, allowing water to transition to a gas.
  • Breaking the covalent bonds within water molecules would require much higher energy, as it involves splitting the molecule into individual hydrogen and oxygen atoms.

Primary and Secondary Bond Types

Bonding can be categorized into primary (strong) and secondary (weak) interactions, each influencing material properties differently.

Primary Bonds

Primary bonds are strong, involving either electron transfer or electron sharing (localized as in a ceramic or delocalized as in a metal). There are 3 primary bond types:

  • Ionic Bonds: Formed when one atom transfers its electron(s) to another atom. Once the transfer occurs, the atoms are now charged and referred to as ions. For example, sodium (Na) with one valence electron (column 1 on periodic table) gives up its electron to chlorine (Cl) which initially has 7 valence electrons (column 17 on periodic table). Now both the sodium ion and chloride ion have 8 valence electrons. This transfer gives both atoms a noble gas configuration (8 valence electrons).
  • Covalent Bonds: A localized bond formed when atoms share electrons with electron orbital overlap. Covalent bonds have a strong angular dependence. For example, in water (H2O), oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogen atoms, allowing all three atoms to mimic noble gas configurations and the angular dependence results in a bond angle of 104.5 degrees.
  • Metallic Bonds: In metals, atoms share a "sea of electrons" as each metal gives up its valence electron(s). This occurs because the valence electrons in metals are loosely bound. This "sea of electrons" or delocalized electrons are now free to move and are why metals make good electrical and thermal conductors.

Secondary Bonds

Secondary bonds are much weaker than primary bonds, and are the result of electrostatic attraction between partially charged atoms. There are two types:

  • Hydrogen Bonds:A least 10 times weaker than metallic bonds, hydrogen bonds result from electrostatic interactions between molecules where hydrogen is bonded to a high electronegative atom (e.g. H2O). The hydrogen bond is formed between molecules whereas within the molecule the hydrogen is bound covalently. See the aside titled "Bonding in Water" for a more detailed description.
  • Van der Waals Forces: Are very weak bonds formed by electrostatic interactions between molecules that have fluctuating charge distribution (dipole). These interactions are important in soft materials such as the interactions between chains of polyethylene, interactions between noble gas molecules, bonding between graphite sheets, and perhaps the adhesion of a Gecko's feet to surfaces.
  • Note of Caution: the bonding between molecules such as methane, CH4 or between chains in polymers such as polyethylene CnH2n+2 are Van der Waals and not hydrogen bonding even though the molecules contain hydrogen. This is because carbon is not a highly electronegative atom and does not result in a charge difference as say oxygen, nitrogen or fluorine would create.

Electron Configuration and Bonding

Electron configuration plays an important role in determining bond types by simply considering the outer electrons (valence electrons). Atoms will tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons resembling that of the nearest noble gas (column 18 on periodic table). Let's take a look at some examples.

The electron configuration notation, such as 1s² 2s² 2p⁶, represents the distribution of electrons in an atom's orbitals, organized by increasing energy levels. The notation breaks down into three parts: the principal quantum number (e.g., 1, 2, 3), which indicates the electron shell or energy level; the subshell type (e.g., s, p, d, f), which specifies the shape of the orbital; and the superscript, which denotes the number of electrons in that subshell.

  • Ionic BondConsider the case of an ionic bond formed when atoms transfer electrons. For example, sodium (Na) with one valence electron easily loses it to chlorine (Cl), which needs one electron to complete its outer shell. This transfer gives both atoms a noble gas configuration.
      NaCl:
      Na has \([Ne] 3s^1\) which is one electron from Ne = \(1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\)
      Cl has \([Ne] 3s^2\ 3p^5\) which is one electron from Ar = \(1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\ 3s^2\ 3p^6\)
      so let's move the one electron from Na to the Cl:
      Na+1 ion = \(1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\)
      Cl-1 ion = \(1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\ 3s^2\ 3p^6\)
  • Covalent Bonds: Formed when atoms share electrons. For example, in water (H2O), oxygen \([He] 2s^2 2p^4\) shares electrons with two hydrogen atoms \(1s^1\), allowing all three atoms to mimic noble gas configurations, Ne and He respectively.
  • Metallic Bonds: In metals, atoms form a "sea of electrons" due to the delocalization of their valence electrons. Unlike in covalent or ionic bonding, metal atoms do not completely give up or share electrons to achieve an "octet." Instead, their outermost s and, in many cases, d orbitals contribute to a "sea" of free-moving electrons. These delocalized electrons are not bound to any specific atom and are free to move throughout the lattice. This mobility of electrons gives metals their high electrical and thermal conductivity, as well as their malleability and ductility. The positively charged atomic nuclei remain fixed in the lattice, held together by the electrostatic attraction to the shared "electron sea."

Metallic Bonding: FCC vs. BCC

Metallic bonding involves delocalized electrons shared across a lattice. The bond strength depends on the electron density and orbital overlap. In FCC metals (e.g., copper, aluminum), bonding is primarily isotropic with moderate cohesive energies (e.g., 300 - 500 kJ/mol). In contrast, BCC metals like tungsten and molybdenum exhibit stronger directional bonding due to contributions from partially filled d-orbitals, resulting in higher cohesive energies (e.g., 400 - 860 kJ/mol for W).

The higher cohesive energies in BCC metals explains their generally higher melting points compared to FCC metals, despite lower packing efficiency.

White Tin vs. Gray Tin

Tin exists in two allotropes (different structural forms), each with distinct bonding and properties:

  • White Tin (\(\beta\)-Tin): Is the metallic structure stable at room temperature. Exhibits metallic bonding with delocalized electrons, leading to a ductile, conductive metal with a cohesive energy of approximately 300 kJ/mol.
  • Gray Tin (\(\alpha\) - Tin): Is the covalent structure stable below 13.2°C. Forms a diamond cubic structure with directional covalent bonds, leading to brittle non conductive solid with but with the same cohesive energy of approximately 300 kJ/mol.

The transition from white tin to gray tin, known as tin pest, involves significant volume expansion and can cause structural failure in tin-based materials in cold environments.

What is Bond Hybridization?

Bond hybridization is the process by which atomic orbitals combine to form new hybrid orbitals that are better suited for bonding. An orbital is a region around an atom where there is a high probability of finding electrons. These hybrid orbitals determine the geometry and strength of chemical bonds.

For example, carbon atoms in diamond undergo sp3-hybridization, where one s-orbital and three p-orbitals mix to form four equivalent tetrahedral bonds. This configuration gives diamond its characteristic hardness.

In contrast, in graphene or graphite, carbon atoms undergo sp2-hybridization, mixing one s-orbital and two p-orbitals, leaving one p-orbital unhybridized. This results in a planar structure with delocalized electrons, which contribute to graphene's exceptional electrical conductivity.

What is Electronegativity?

Electronegativity is a measure of an atom's ability to attract and hold onto electrons within a chemical bond. It is a fundamental concept that helps explain the nature of chemical bonding and the behavior of molecules.

  • High Electronegativity: Atoms with high electronegativity (e.g. columns 16,17 on periodic table), such as oxygen or fluorine, have a strong tendency to attract electrons toward themselves.
  • Low Electronegativity: Atoms with low electronegativity (e.g. columns 1,2 on periodic table), like sodium or potassium, are less able to attract electrons and often lose them in bonding.

The difference in electronegativity between two bonded atoms determines the type of bond formed:

  • Ionic Bonds: Form when the electronegativity difference is large (>1.7), causing one atom to completely transfer electrons to the other (e.g., NaCl).
  • Covalent Bonds: Form when the electronegativity difference is small (<0.5), leading to shared electrons between atoms (e.g., H2).
  • Polar Covalent Bonds: Occur when the difference is moderate (0.5 - 1.7), resulting in uneven electron sharing and partial charges (e.g., H2O).

Electronegativity is quantified on the Pauling scale, where fluorine is the most electronegative element with a value of 3.98, and cesium is one of the least electronegative with a value of 0.79.

Electronegativity Differences and Bond Types

In general, electronegativity differences can be categorized as follows:

  • Low Electronegativity Difference (< 0.5): Indicates nonpolar covalent bonds, where electrons are shared nearly equally between the atoms.
    Example: CH4 (methane), with a difference of |2.55 - 2.20| = 0.35.
  • Moderate Electronegativity Difference (0.5–1.7): Indicates polar covalent bonds, where electrons are shared unequally, resulting in partial charges.
    Example: H2O (water), with a difference of |3.44 - 2.20| = 1.24.
  • High Electronegativity Difference (> 1.7): Indicates ionic bonds, where electrons are transferred from one atom to another, forming ions.
    Example: NaCl, with a difference of |3.16 - 0.93| = 2.23.

These cutoff values are provided only as a general rule of thumb.

Percent Ionic Character Example

The percent ionic character of a bond (the remainder being covalent) can be estimated using the electronegativity difference (ΔEN) and the following formula:

Percent Ionic Character = [1 - exp(-0.25 (ΔEN)2)] × 100

  • NaCl:
    ΔEN = |3.16 - 0.93| = 2.23
    Percent Ionic Character = [1 - exp(-0.25 (2.23)2)] × 100
    = [1 - exp(-1.243)] × 100
    = [1 - 0.288] × 100
    = 71.2%
  • H-O Bond (Water):
    ΔEN = |3.44 - 2.20| = 1.24
    Percent Ionic Character = [1 - exp(-0.25 (1.24)2)] × 100
    = [1 - exp(-0.3841)] × 100
    = [1 - 0.681] × 100
    = 31.9%
  • C-H Bond (Methane):
    ΔEN = |2.55 - 2.20| = 0.35
    Percent Ionic Character = [1 - exp(-0.25 (0.35)2)] × 100
    = [1 - exp(-0.030625)] × 100
    = [1 - 0.9698] × 100
    = 3.0%

These examples demonstrate the varying ionic character of different bonds: NaCl has ionic bonding (~71% ionic, 29% covalent); The H-O bond in water is covalent (68%) but significantly polar (~32% ionic); and The C-H bond in methane is essentially completely covalent (97%) with only a 3.0% ionic character.
