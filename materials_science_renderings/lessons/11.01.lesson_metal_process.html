Introduction to Metal Processing

Metal processing involves a variety of techniques to shape, modify, and enhance the properties of metals. Understanding these processes is crucial for materials engineers to tailor metal characteristics for specific applications.

Learning Objectives

  1. Identify and explain different metal processing techniques.
  2. Understand the effects of processing on microstructure and mechanical properties of metals.
  3. Describe key metalworking processes like casting, forging, rolling, extrusion, and machining.
  4. Analyze the impact of heat treatment on metal properties.

Metal Processing Techniques

Casting

Casting is the process of pouring molten metal into a mold, allowing it to solidify into a specific shape. This technique is used for producing large, complex components and can be performed using various methods:

  • Sand Casting: Ideal for large, complex parts. The mold is made of sand and destroyed after each use.
  • Die Casting: Involves high-pressure injection of molten metal into a steel mold, suitable for high-volume production.
  • Investment Casting: Uses a wax pattern coated in ceramic. After the wax is melted away, metal is poured into the cavity.
  • Continuous Casting: Produces long sections of metal, such as rods and sheets, with consistent quality.

Forging

Forging shapes metal through compressive forces, often at high temperatures. It improves the mechanical properties of the metal by refining the grain structure.

  • Open-Die Forging: The metal is compressed between flat dies, allowing for free flow and large deformations.
  • Closed-Die Forging: The metal is confined within shaped dies, resulting in a precise final shape.
  • Cold vs. Hot Forging: Cold forging increases strength through strain hardening, while hot forging allows easier deformation.

Rolling

Rolling reduces the thickness of metal sheets by passing them through rotating rolls. It can be performed at different temperatures:

  • Hot Rolling: Performed above the recrystallization temperature, resulting in larger grain sizes and a rough surface finish.
  • Cold Rolling: Performed below the recrystallization temperature, increasing strength and providing a smoother finish.

Extrusion

Extrusion involves forcing a metal billet through a die to create a long product with a specific cross-sectional shape. It can be performed hot or cold, depending on the material and desired properties.

  • Direct Extrusion: The billet is pushed through the die by a ram.
  • Indirect Extrusion: The die moves towards the stationary billet, reducing the required force.

Machining

Machining removes material from a workpiece to achieve a precise shape. Common techniques include:

  • Turning: The workpiece rotates while a cutting tool removes material.
  • Milling: A rotating cutting tool moves across the surface, shaping the material.
  • Drilling: Creates cylindrical holes in the workpiece.

Heat Treatment

Heat treatment involves heating and cooling a metal to alter its microstructure and properties. Common processes include:

  • Annealing: Softens the metal and relieves internal stresses.
  • Quenching: Rapid cooling increases hardness but can introduce brittleness.
  • Tempering: Reheating quenched metal to reduce brittleness while maintaining hardness.
  • Case Hardening: Increases surface hardness while keeping the core tough.

Additive Manufacturing: Friction Stir Deposition

Friction Stir Deposition (FSD) is a solid-state additive manufacturing process that combines the principles of friction stir welding with material deposition. It is an innovative technique used for additive manufacturing, repair, and coating applications, particularly with metals.

  • Solid-State Process: Unlike traditional additive manufacturing, FSD does not melt the material. Instead, a rotating tool generates heat through friction, softening the metal and allowing it to be deposited layer by layer.
  • High Bond Strength: The process produces strong metallurgical bonds due to mechanical stirring and pressure, resulting in excellent adhesion and minimal porosity.
  • Material Versatility: FSD can be used with a wide range of metals, including aluminum, copper, and titanium alloys, as well as dissimilar metal combinations.
  • Applications: Commonly used for surface coatings, part repair, and manufacturing of complex geometries, especially in the aerospace and automotive industries.

Summary

Metal processing techniques are fundamental in shaping and enhancing the properties of metals. Each method has specific advantages and applications, allowing engineers to tailor materials for various industries. The processing methods highlighted in this lesson are only a few of the many processing methods used today.

Practice Problems

  1. Describe the microstructural changes that occur during hot rolling of steel.
  2. Calculate the force required to extrude an aluminum rod from a diameter of 20 mm to 10 mm. Assume a yield strength of 150 MPa.
  3. Explain the benefits of using closed-die forging for aerospace components.
