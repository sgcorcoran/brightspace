The Three Bravais Lattices of the Cubic Crystal System

The cubic crystal system has three Bravais lattices: simle cubic (sc), body-centered cubic (bcc) and face-centered cubic (fcc) as shown above. To create a specific crystal structure, we need to associate a basis of atoms with each lattice point. The least complicated crystal structure (typical of metals) would be to place a single atom type at each point. For example, iron is a bcc lattice with one iron atom located at each lattice point as represented in the figure below.

Terminology: Unit Cell

The unit cell is the smallest unit that when repeated side-by-side in 3 dimensions forms the overall crystalline structure. The least complicated unit cell is composed of a Bravais lattice with only one atom located at each lattice point. The cut-sphere model is the best representation of a unit cell since it shows only the the part of the atoms within the cell, i.e the corners contain 1/8 of an atom while the faces contain 1/2 of an atom.

By associating 1 atom with each point of the sc, bcc, and fcc lattices, we can generate 3 distinct cubic crystal structures as shown above. When viewing these cells, see if you can identify the directions in which the atoms touch e.g. along edges, along body diagonals, or along face diagonals. Also notice that the total number of atoms in each unit cell (and the volume of each cell) increases. By counting corners as 1/8 of an atom, faces as 1/2 of an atom, and the body center as 1 atom, we find that the simple cubic cell contains 1 full atom; the body-centered structure contains 2 atoms; and the face-centered structure contains 4 atoms.

The radius of the atoms used in these illustrations were kept constant to accurately reflect the relative sizes of each unit cell. However, notice that the size of the fcc unit cell is not 4 times the size of the sc cell (more about this later) even though it contains 4 times the number of atoms.

Compare SC and BCC unit cells

The sc cell contains 1 atom while the bcc cell contains 2 atoms but the cell volume does not double in size. In fact, it is only 1.54 times larger in volume. This implies that the packing efficiency (and therefore density) must be greater for the bcc lattice as compared to the SC lattice. We can quantify this by calculating the percent volume of each unit cell taken up by atoms. The atoms in the simple cubic lattice take up 50% of the volume while in the bcc lattice atoms take up 68% of the volume. This packing efficiency is usually expressed in fractional form and referred to as the atomic packing factor (APF). Thus, we would say that the body-centered cubic structure has an APF of 0.68.

Concept check

Consider 3 samples in the shape of a cube each containing 1 million atoms and labeled A, B, and C. Sample A has a simple cubic crystal structure while sample B is body-centered cubic and sample C is face-centered cubic. Which sample would measure as the largest? Smallest? Which sample would weigh the most?
