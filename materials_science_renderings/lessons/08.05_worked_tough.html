Worked Example: Toughness and Fracture Energy Calculation

Determine the toughness and fracture energy for a metallic alloy and a polymer under impact loading.

Objectives:

  1. Calculate the toughness of a material using the area under the stress-strain curve.
  2. Determine the fracture energy using Charpy impact test data.

Key Concept: Toughness and Fracture Energy

Toughness is a measure of a material's ability to absorb energy before fracturing. It is represented by the area under the stress-strain curve. Fracture energy (\( G \)) quantifies the energy required to propagate a crack and is often measured using impact tests, such as the Charpy test.

\[ G = \frac{\text{Impact Energy}}{A} \]

Where \( G \) is the fracture energy and \( A \) is the cross-sectional area of the sample.

Expanded Problem Statement

Determine the toughness and fracture energy for:

  • A steel sample with a yield stress of \( 400 \, \text{MPa} \), ultimate tensile strength of \( 600 \, \text{MPa} \), and fracture strain of \( 0.2 \). The impact energy from a Charpy test is \( 150 \, \text{J} \), and the cross-sectional area is \( 0.005 \, \text{m}^2 \).
  • A polycarbonate sample with a yield stress of \( 70 \, \text{MPa} \), ultimate tensile strength of \( 80 \, \text{MPa} \), and fracture strain of \( 0.5 \). The impact energy is \( 20 \, \text{J} \), with a cross-sectional area of \( 0.001 \, \text{m}^2 \).

Solution

Step 1: Calculate Toughness Using the Stress-Strain Curve

Metallic Material: Steel

The toughness is the area under the stress-strain curve, approximated by the trapezoidal rule:

\[ \text{Toughness} = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_{\text{yield}} + \sigma_{\text{UTS}}) \times \epsilon_f = \frac{1}{2}(400 + 600) \times 0.2 = 100 \, \text{MPa} \]

Polymeric Material: Polycarbonate

\[ \text{Toughness} = \frac{1}{2}(70 + 80) \times 0.5 = 37.5 \, \text{MPa} \]

Step 2: Calculate Fracture Energy Using Charpy Impact Test Data

Steel

\[ G = \frac{\text{Impact Energy}}{A} = \frac{150 \, \text{J}}{0.005 \, \text{m}^2} = 30 \, \text{kJ/m}^2 \]

Polycarbonate

\[ G = \frac{20 \, \text{J}}{0.001 \, \text{m}^2} = 20 \, \text{kJ/m}^2 \]

Step 3: Interpretation of Results

Steel exhibits higher toughness and fracture energy, indicating better resistance to crack propagation compared to polycarbonate.
