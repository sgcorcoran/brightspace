Phase Diagrams Part 2: Tie Line and Lever Rule

The Tie Line and Lever Rule Explained

Analogy: The Seesaw

Understanding the tie line and lever rule can be simplified using the analogy of a seesaw. Imagine two individuals sitting on opposite ends of a seesaw:

  • The fulcrum (balance point) represents the overall composition \( C_o \) of your alloy.
  • The two individuals represent the compositions of the two phases present at a specific temperature, \( C_\alpha \) and \( C_L \).
  • The distances from the fulcrum to each person represent the differences in compositions.
  • The weights (masses) placed at each end represent the weight fractions (\( W_\alpha \) and \( W_L \)) of the two phases.

Mass Balance Derivation of the Lever Rule

Consider an alloy with overall composition \( C_o \) that separates into two phases, α and liquid (L), with compositions \( C_\alpha \) and \( C_L \), respectively. Let \( W_\alpha \) and \( W_L \) be their mass fractions. The mass balance for component concentration is:

\[ W_\alpha C_\alpha + W_L C_L = C_o \]

Since the total mass must equal unity:

\[ W_\alpha + W_L = 1 \]

Solving these equations simultaneously for \( W_\alpha \) and \( W_L \) gives:

\[ W_\alpha = \frac{C_o - C_L}{C_\alpha - C_L} \quad \text{and} \quad W_L = \frac{C_\alpha - C_o}{C_\alpha - C_L} \]
