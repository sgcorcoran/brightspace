Fick’s Second Law: Non-Steady-State Diffusion

Fick’s Second Law describes how diffusion concentration **changes over time**. It is given by:

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \]

where:

  • \(C\) = Concentration.
  • \(t\) = Time.
  • \(D\) = Diffusion coefficient.
  • \(\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2}\) = Change in concentration over distance.

It is commonly applied in **heat treatment, doping semiconductors, and gas diffusion in solids**.

Solving Diffusion Problems

Let’s work through an example applying Fick’s Second Law.

Example: Carburization of Steel

In carburization, carbon atoms diffuse into the surface of steel to increase hardness. The solution to Fick’s Second Law in this case is:

\[ C(x,t) = C_s + (C_0 - C_s) \text{erf} \left( \frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}} \right) \]

  • \(C(x,t)\) = Concentration at depth \(x\) and time \(t\).
  • \(C_s\) = Surface concentration.
  • \(C_0\) = Initial concentration.
  • \(\text{erf}\) = Error function.

Industrial Applications of Diffusion

Diffusion plays a critical role in several industrial processes:

  • Semiconductors: Doping silicon to create electronic components.
  • Metallurgy: Case hardening of gears for wear resistance.
  • Corrosion Protection: Diffusion barriers in coatings.
