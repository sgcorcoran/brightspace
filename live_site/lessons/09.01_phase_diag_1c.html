Phase Diagrams Part 2: Phase Diagram Interpretation

Interpreting Binary Isomorphous Phase Diagrams

A binary isomorphous phase diagram describes alloy systems with complete solubility between two components in both liquid and solid phases. Here, we will explore how to interpret these diagrams, specifically using the Cu-Ni alloy system as an example.

Step-by-Step Analysis at Different Temperatures

1. Identifying Phases at Specific Temperatures

Consider an alloy with 35 wt% Ni:

  • At 1300°C: Alloy is fully liquid (single-phase region).
  • At 1250°C: Alloy enters a two-phase region (α + Liquid).
  • At 1190°C: Alloy is completely solid (α phase).

2. Determining Phase Compositions (Using Tie Lines)

To find the composition of phases at a given temperature, draw a horizontal line (tie line) across the two-phase region:

  • At 1250°C:
    • Liquid phase composition (intersection with liquidus line): ~32 wt% Ni
    • Solid phase composition (intersection with solidus line): ~43 wt% Ni

3. Calculating Phase Amounts (Lever Rule)

The lever rule is used to calculate the relative amounts of each phase:

For alloy at 1250°C with overall composition of 35 wt% Ni:

  • Fraction Liquid (\(W_L\)): \[ W_L = \frac{C_{α}-C_o}{C_{α}-C_L} = \frac{43-35}{43-32} = \frac{8}{11} = 0.73 \text{ (73% liquid)} \]
  • Fraction Solid (\(W_{α}\)): \[ W_{α} = \frac{C_o - C_L}{C_{α}-C_L} = \frac{35-32}{43-32} = \frac{3}{11} = 0.27 \text{ (27% solid)} \]

Microstructural Implications

The relative amounts and compositions of phases strongly influence the final microstructure of the alloy upon cooling:

  • Single-phase regions: Uniform microstructures with homogenous properties.
  • Two-phase regions: Complex microstructures, properties dependent on cooling rates and phase distribution.
