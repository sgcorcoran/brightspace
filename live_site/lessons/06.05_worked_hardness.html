Worked Example: Hardness and Tensile Strength Correlation

Estimate the tensile strength of a material using hardness measurements for both a metallic alloy and a polymer.

Objectives:

  1. Understand the empirical relationship between hardness and tensile strength.
  2. Estimate tensile strength based on hardness values for different materials.

Key Concept: Hardness-Tensile Strength Correlation

Hardness is a measure of a material's resistance to localized deformation. There is an empirical correlation between hardness (e.g., Brinell or Vickers hardness) and tensile strength (\( \sigma_t \)). For many metals, the relationship is approximated by:

\[ \sigma_t \approx k \times \text{Hardness} \]

Where \( k \) is a material-specific constant. For polymers, this relationship is less direct and can be influenced by viscoelastic effects.

Expanded Problem Statement

Determine the tensile strength for:

  • An aluminum alloy with a Vickers hardness of \( 150 \, \text{HV} \), using \( k = 3.3 \).
  • A nylon sample with a Shore D hardness of \( 70 \), using an approximate correlation \( \sigma_t \approx 0.1 \times \text{Hardness} \) (MPa for Shore D scale).

Solution

Metallic Alloy: Aluminum

Use the empirical relationship for metals:

\[ \sigma_t = k \times \text{Hardness} = 3.3 \times 150 = 495 \, \text{MPa} \]

Polymeric Material: Nylon

Use the approximate correlation for Shore D hardness:

\[ \sigma_t = 0.1 \times 70 = 7 \, \text{MPa} \]

How reliable is the hardness-tensile strength correlation?

Discuss factors that might affect the accuracy of this empirical relationship, particularly for polymers.
