Worked Example: Brittle Fracture Using Griffith's Criterion

Determine the critical stress for the fracture of glass in air versus glass in water using Griffith's criterion.

Objectives:

  1. Introduce Griffith's criterion for brittle fracture.
  2. Calculate the critical stress for fracture of glass in air.
  3. Compare the results for glass in air and glass in water.

Background on Griffith's Criterion

Terminology: Griffith's Criterion

Griffith's criterion states that brittle fracture occurs when the energy released by the formation of new surfaces is equal to the elastic strain energy stored in the material. The critical stress for crack propagation is given by: \[ \sigma_c = \sqrt{\frac{2 E \gamma}{\pi a}} \] where:

  • \(\sigma_c\) is the critical stress for fracture.
  • \(E\) is the Young's modulus of the material.
  • \(\gamma\) is the surface energy (J/m2).
  • \(a\) is the crack length (m).

Worked Solution: Glass in Air

Given:
  • Young's modulus of glass: \(E = 70 \, \text{GPa}\)
  • Surface energy in air: \(\gamma_{\text{air}} = 0.3 \, \text{J/m}^2\)
  • Crack length: \(a = 0.001 \, \text{m}\)
Solution:
Using Griffith's equation: \[ \sigma_c = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 70 \times 10^9 \times 0.3}{\pi \times 0.001}} \] \[ \sigma_c \approx 2.06 \, \text{MPa} \]

Worked Solution: Glass in Water

Given:
  • Young's modulus of glass: \(E = 70 \, \text{GPa}\)
  • Surface energy in water: \(\gamma_{\text{water}} = 0.015 \, \text{J/m}^2\)
  • Crack length: \(a = 0.001 \, \text{m}\)
Solution:
Using Griffith's equation: \[ \sigma_c = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 70 \times 10^9 \times 0.015}{\pi \times 0.001}} \] \[ \sigma_c \approx 0.53 \, \text{MPa} \]
