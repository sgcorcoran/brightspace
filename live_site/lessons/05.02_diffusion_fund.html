Introduction to Diffusion

Diffusion is the process by which atoms move in solids. This process can happen by two mechanisms either vacancy diffusion or interstitial diffusion. In this chapter, the net diffusion of atoms will be driven by a concentration gradient, i.e. a difference in concentration of a diffusing species. The net movement of diffusing species will be from the high concentration regions toward the low concentration regions. This process is critical in many applications. In particular, we will discuss:

  • Doping of semiconductor materials.
  • The movement of gas through a thin wall containeer.
  • The deleterious effect of segregation of species to grain boundaries.
  • Hardening of metal surfaces by carburization.

The Role of Diffusion in Ancient Sword Making

During the early Iron Age (c. 1200 BCE – 500 CE), blacksmiths developed advanced metalworking techniques to improve sword strength and durability. One such technique, pattern welding, combined alternating layers of iron and steel through repeated forging, twisting, and welding. This process not only increased flexibility and toughness but also created distinctive surface patterns that were enhanced by etching. This process was unintentionally optimized by early blacksmiths, long before the scientific understanding of diffusion.

Modern metallurgical studies confirm that repeated forging and carbon diffusion strengthened these weapons, making pattern-welding an early example of applied materials science.

Mechanisms of Diffusion

Vacancy Diffusion

Atoms move by hopping into vacant lattice sites. We often speak of this as the diffusion of vacancies but it is really the movement of atoms into the vacancy that results in the movement of mass. This mechanism was shown in the second video above. The is common in substitutional metal alloys such as Cu-Zn, Cu-Ni, Ag-Au and any alloy system where the atoms are relatively close in size.

Interstitial Diffusion

Interstital diffusion occurs in interstial alloys such as steel (carbon in iron). Here the diffusing species is much much smaller that the host atoms and so occupy interstitial sites. Interstitial diffusion tends to be faster than vacancy diffusion for two reasons: (1) the atoms are smaller and move faster and (2) the atoms can hop into any neighboring interstitial site. In contrast, atoms in the vacancy mechanism can only move if a vacancy exists near it.

Fick’s First Law: Steady-State Diffusion

Fick's 1st law describes the observation that the net movement of a diffusing species occurs down a concentration gradient or in mathematical terms this rate would be proportional to \(-\frac{dC}{dx}\) for diffusion in one direction.

\[ J = -D \frac{dC}{dx} \]

  • \(J\) = Diffusion flux, \(\frac{kg}{m^2 sec}\).
  • \(D\) = Diffusion coefficient (Diffusivity), \(\frac{m^2}{sec}\).
  • \(\frac{dC}{dx}\) = Concentration gradient, \(\frac{kg}{m^4}\)

For the case of diffusion in the steady state (or through a thin membrane), the concentration gradient is linear and we can write:

\[ \frac{dC}{dx} = \frac{\Delta C}{\Delta x} = \frac{C_2-C_1}{x_2-x_1} \]

\[ J = -D \frac{C_2-C_1}{x_2-x_1} \]

Calculation: Steady-State Diffusion

Calculate the steady state diffusion rate of helium through pyrex at 500 °C in units of liters / hour, given:

  • The diffusivity of helium in pyrex = \(2\times10^{-8} \frac{cm^2}{sec}\) at 500 °C
  • The pyrex wall thickness is 0.5 mm with an area of 10 \(cm^2\)
  • The concentration of helium on the high concentration and low concentration surfaces of the pyrex are \(10^{-2} \frac{kg}{m^3}\) and \(0 \frac{kg}{m^3}\) repectively.
  • The density of helium at STP is 0.1785 g/l.

Our plan is to use Fick's 1st law to solve for the grams of helium that diffusives through the pyrex per area of pyrex per second (being careful with units). We will use the known area to get grams per second and then to convert grams to liters using the given density.

Use Fick's law and solve

  • From Fick's 1st law we have (after converting units): \[ J = -D \frac{C_2-C_1}{x_2-x_1}=-\left(2\times10^{-8} cm^2\,sec^{-1}\right) \frac{0\frac{kg}{m^3}-10^{-2} \frac{kg}{m^3}}{0.5 \,mm}=4\times 10^{-8} \frac{g}{m^2 sec} \]
  • This answer is a quantity, g, per area per time. We want a quantity, liters, per time (no area). We will (a) multiply by the area and (b) convert g to liters. \[4\times 10^{-8} \frac{g}{m^2 sec}\times \frac{\text{Area}}{\text{density at STP}}=4\times 10^{-8} \frac{g}{m^2 sec}\times \frac{10 \,cm^2}{0.1785 \,g/liters}\]
  • Multiplying and converting \(cm^2\) to \(m^2\) we get: \[=2.2\times 10^{-10} \,\frac{liters}{sec}\]

Factors Affecting Diffusion

Temperature

From Fick's 1st law, we now know that \(J = -D \frac{C_2-C_1}{x_2-x_1}\). From this equation, it's not obvious that there is a strong temperature dependence of the flux, \(J\). The temperature dependence comes from the Diffusivity term, \(D\), which is given as:

\[ D = D_0 \,e^{-\frac{Q}{RT}} \]

where the pre-exponential, \(D_0\), and the activation energy, \(Q\), are fitting parameters and are independent of temperature. \(R\) is the gas constant and \(T\) is the absolute temperature in Kelvin. Concentration Gradient

While temperature affects the rate of diffusion exponentially, concentration affects the rate only linearly as given directly from Fick's 1st law. It is usually easier in practice to change the wall thickness (denominator) to change the concentration gradient.

Area

The flux as calculated is independent of area as it is defined on a per area basis. However, when we are interested in maximizing a process that requires the movement of a gas through a membrane then increasing the total area will increase the total quantity of substance that moves through. Instead of flat plates, we could move to an array of thin walled tubes and significantly increase the total throughput.

Crystal Structure & Defects

More open structures such as in the bcc system allow for faster diffusion as compared to densely packed structures such as fcc. Defects that are "more open" than the lattice also allows for faster diffusion rates. Examples would be grain boundaries, surfaces and dislocation cores. Polymer materials also have much more open structures as compared to metals something we will discuss further in a future chapter.

Which diffusion mechanism is faster, vacancy or interstitial? Why?

Interstitial diffusion is faster because (a) smaller atoms move faster and (b) all neighboring sites are available for an interstitial while a vacancy has to be present for an atom to move in the vacancy mechanism.

Which factor affects diffusion rates more: temperature or concentration gradients?

Temperature since it is exponential while the concentration gradient effect is only linear.

Do you expect an fcc or bcc material to have faster diffusion rates if all other factors are the same.

BCC because bcc materials are less dense and therefore have more room for diffusion.
