Introduction to Strengthening Mechanisms

Strengthening mechanisms refers to the methods used to increase the resistance of a material to deformation. This is crucial in engineering applications where structural integrity and performance depend on mechanical strength. Below are some broad generalizations for the strengthening of metals, polymers, and ceramics. We will address metals in this module and later in the course we will discuss polymer and ceramic strengthening.

  • Metals: Strengthened by mechanisms that affect the movement of dislocations.
  • Polymers: Strengthened by mechanisms that change the interactions between polymer chains.
  • Ceramics: Strengthened by mechanisms that deflect or prevent crack growth.

Dislocations and Their Role in Strengthening Metals

Dislocations are linear defects in a crystal structure that enable plastic deformation i.e. without dislocations our material would be brittle. Strengthening methods hinder the motion of dislocations resulting in an increase a material’s resistance to deformation. As we move further into our discussion of mechanisms, ask yourself, "How are dislocations being hindered in this mechanism?" Before we get to mechanisms, we need to review dislocations briefly.

Types of Dislocations

Refer to module 5 on defects to learn more about dislocations.

  • Edge Dislocation: Extra half-plane of atoms distorting the crystal structure along a line.
  • Screw Dislocation: Spiral distortion of atoms along a line.
  • Mixed Dislocation: A combination of both edge and screw.

Dislocation Motion

The figure above shows two different mechanisms for plastic deformation. The top row shows the movement of a dislocation across a plane resulting in the displacement of the atoms one atomic distance to the right. The bottom mechanism also results in a displacement of one atomic distance to the right but this mechanism hass the entire plane sliding at one time. Although both mechanisms result in permanent deformation, the stress required for the dislocation mehanism is 10 times smaller than that required for the plane sliding mechanism. In fact, the plane sliding mechanism requires a stress very close to the theoretical strength of the material meaning that the material is more likely to fracture in a brittle manner than to plastically deform. Further, experiments confirm that plastic deformation occurs at stress values much, much lower than the theoretical strength.

Why are fcc materials more ductile than hcp materials?

For the fcc system, we have a total of 12 slip systems that are oriented uniformly around the unit cell. No matter which direction stress is applied there will always be active slip systems. For the hcp system, we have only 6 slip systems (see the figure below) and their orientation is not uniform. Overall it is more difficult to activate slip under these conditions resulting in a less ductile material.

Does this mean that bcc is more ductile than fcc since bcc has 24 systems compared to 12 in fcc? No.

No. There is an additional factor called the Peierls stress that represents a friction stress resisting dislocation motion. In bcc metals, this friction stress is significantly larger (100x) than that of the fcc metals.

Experimental observations

The dislocations in the image (pyramid shaped pits) are moving toward the left due to the applied shear stress. Notice that the dislocations have been stopped by a grain boundary and are "piling-up." The dislocations are not merging to form double or triple dislocation cores but remain separate. The spacing near the grain boundary is smaller than that away from the boundary as expected for defects that repel each other. Notice that the dislocations also appear to move along specific directions i.e. the slip directions as discussed in the previous section.

Conclusions based on the figure above.

  • Grain boundaries can block dislocation motion.
  • Dislocations repel each other.
  • Dislocations move in specific directions.
