Phase Diagrams Part 2: Interpretation and Applications

Interpreting Binary Phase Diagrams

Understanding a binary phase diagram involves identifying the phases present, their compositions, and the relative amounts (mass fractions) at various temperatures and compositions.

Step-by-Step Example (Cu-Ni Alloy)

Consider a Cu-Ni alloy with 35 wt% Ni at different temperatures:

  • 1300°C: One phase - liquid solution (L).
  • 1250°C: Two phases - solid solution (α) and liquid solution (L).
  • 1190°C: One phase - solid solution (α).

Calculating Phase Compositions and Fractions

Use the lever rule and tie lines to determine the phase compositions and fractions. Example:

  • At 1250°C, the liquid phase composition is ~32% Ni, and the solid phase is ~43% Ni.
  • Weight fraction calculations:
    \[ W_L = \frac{43-35}{43-32} = 0.73 \] (73% liquid)
    \[ W_α = \frac{35-32}{43-32} = 0.27 \] (27% solid)

Microstructural Analysis During Cooling

As alloys cool, microstructures evolve based on the phase diagrams:

  • At 1260°C, first solid forms (α phase).
  • At 1220°C, the alloy fully solidifies.

Evaluating Alloy Cooling Histories

Analyze scenarios to predict microstructural outcomes:

  • Slow cooling: Larger grain size, clearly defined phases.
  • Rapid cooling: Smaller grain size, potential metastable phases.
