Non-steady state diffusion

Introduction

Consider a slab of material such as Fe with the surface coated in another substance for example graphite. Once heated the carbon from the graphite will diffuse into the iron slab. As time continues, the carbon will diffuse further and further into the iron. The animation below shows this process. Notice that the concentration profile is no longer linear and it continues to change with time. This is a non-steady state process i.e. the concentration at any position changes as time progresses.

Carbon diffusion into iron

The animation above shows a non-steady state diffusion process. If this was carbon diffusing into iron, the process would be called carburization and is used to increase the hardness and wear resistance of iron or steel. To predict the concentration of carbon at a specific depth and time, we need to apply Fick's 2nd law: \[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \]

Physical Interpretation

Fick's 2nd law says that at any fixed position in our material, the local concentration changes with time according to the 2nd derivative of the concentration. This quantifies how the concentration gradient (Fick's 1st law) changes in the neighborhood of our position of interest. If we had a linear concentration gradient as in the steady state case, the 2nd derivative would be zero and the concentration change with time would be zero. However, if we do not have a linear gradient than the concentration will change with time. The solution of Fick's 2nd law for the case of carburization (semi-infinite diffusion) is given below.

Semi-infinite diffusion (e.g. carburization)

To solve Fick’s Second Law, we must apply boundary conditions that describe how the concentration varies at the system’s boundaries. For the case of semi-infinite diffusion we have:

  • Constant surface concentration: The surface concentration remains fixed at (\(C_s\)).
  • No change in concentration deep in the material: \(C_∞=C_0\)
  • An initial concentration: \(C_0\)

Solving Fick's 2nd law with the above boundary conditions gives:

\[ \frac{C_{(x,t)}-C_0}{C_s-C_0} = 1 - \text{erf} \left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}} \right) \]

where:

  • \(C_{(x,t)}\) = Carbon concentration at depth \(x\) and time \(t\).
  • \(C_s\) = Surface carbon concentration.
  • \(C_0\) = Initial concentration
  • \(\text{erf}\) = Error function.
  • \(D\) = Diffusion coefficient.
  • \(x\) = Depth.
  • \(t\) = Time.

Example Calculation

A steel surface is exposed to a high-carbon environment at \( 1000^\circ C \). The carbon concentration at the surface is maintained at \(0.8\%\) by weight, while the initial concentration inside the material is \(0.2\%\). Determine the carbon concentration at a depth of \(0.5\, mm\) after \(2\) hours.

Step 1: Identify Parameters

  • \( C_s = 0.8\% \)
  • \( C_0 = 0.2\% \)
  • \( x = 0.5 \text{ mm} = 5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m} \)
  • \( D = 1.9 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^2/\text{s} \) (at 1000 °C)
  • \( t = 2 \times 3600 \text{ s} \)

Step 2: Compute Dimensionless Variable

\[ \frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}} = \frac{5 \times 10^{-4}}{2\sqrt{(1.9 \times 10^{-11} \times 7200)}} \]

Solving, we find \(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}} \approx 0.6759\).

\[ \frac{C_{(x,t)}-0.2}{0.8-0.2} = 1 - \text{erf} \left(0.6759 \right) \]

Step 3: Use Error Function Approximation

Using an error function table, we find \(\text{erf}(0.6759) \approx 0.661\), giving:

\[ \frac{C_{(x,t)}-0.2}{0.8-0.2} = 1 - 0.661 \]

\( C_{(x,t)} \approx 0.40\% \).

The error function can be computed in Excel by typing: =ERF().
