Phase Diagrams Part 2: Microstructural Analysis During Cooling

Microstructural Changes in Alloys During Cooling

As alloys cool from the liquid phase through the solidification process, their microstructure evolves dramatically. Understanding these microstructural transformations helps predict and control alloy properties.

Stages of Microstructural Development

Let's examine the detailed stages during the cooling of a 35 wt% Ni alloy in the Cu-Ni system:

1. Initial Solid Formation

As the temperature drops to approximately 1260°C, the first solid (α phase) begins to nucleate from the liquid. This initial solid forms as dendrites due to the directional growth influenced by heat flow.

2. Growth of Solid Phase

Upon further cooling, dendrites grow larger and more complex. The dendritic structures become clearer, and the remaining liquid enriches in the other component (Ni in this case).

3. Solidification Completion

At approximately 1220°C, solidification is complete, resulting in a fully solid alloy. The final microstructure typically contains distinct dendritic grains and interdendritic regions that reflect the solute redistribution.

Influence of Cooling Rate on Microstructure

The cooling rate significantly affects the alloy microstructure:

  • Slow cooling: Results in coarse dendrites and clearly defined grain boundaries. Slow cooling typically leads to improved ductility and toughness.
  • Rapid cooling: Results in finer dendritic structures, smaller grains, and less defined grain boundaries. Rapidly cooled alloys tend to have higher strength and hardness but may become more brittle.
