Defects in Materials

Introduction to Materials Defects

Defects in materials, which are deviations from a perfect crystalline structure, play a fundamental role in shaping materials properties and performance. These imperfections are not merely flaws but are often deliberately manipulated to enhance mechanical, thermal, and functional characteristics.

Point Defects

Point defects are localized disruptions in the crystal structure that occur at a single atomic site or involve a small group of atoms. These defects can significantly impact material properties such as electrical conductivity, strength, and diffusion. Common types of point defects include:

  • Vacancies: Sites in the lattice where an atom is missing. These defects play a role in diffusion as atoms can jump into these empty spaces.
  • Interstitials: Extra atoms positioned at non-lattice sites within the crystal. These are usually smaller atoms squeezed into spaces between larger atoms for example carbon in iron.
  • Substitutional Impurities: Atoms of a different type that replace atoms in the lattice for example in brass, zinc substitutes for copper atoms. This type of defect is common in alloys and is used to enhance material properties.

There is also a self-interstitial. This would be a host atom, located at an interstitial site rather than at a regular lattice site. These are not so common as the host atom is quite large compared to the interstitial spaces. Self-interstitials can be observed in radiation damaged materials.

Linear Defects (Dislocations)

Dislocations are line defects that play a critical role in plastic (permanent) deformation and material strength.

Edge Dislocation Motion

  • Edge Dislocations: An extra half-plane of atoms inserted into the lattice. An edge dislocation moves parallel to the direction the crystal is displaced. The video above shows the concept of how an edge dislocation would move through a material.
  • Screw Dislocations: A helical ramp that moves perpendicular to the direction the crystal is displaced.

Most dislocations have a mixed character. A pure edge and pure screw dislocation are oriented 90 degrees from each other as shown in the video below (mixed dislocation motion). The left face in this video has a pure screw dislocation while the front face has a pure edge. The curved line of the dislocation is a mix of the two. Notice in this video that as the dislocation line moves through the crystal, the top half of the crystal is shifted one atomic spacing relative to the bottom half and creating a step at the surface.

TEM Movie: Dislocation Motion

In the second video, a real dislocation is observed moving in silicon at elevated temperature while a stress is applied. The video was taken using an electron transmission microscope (TEM). Notice how the dislocation looks like a curved line and as it moves it leaves behind a step on the surface (dark lines).

Dislocations are difficult to visualize so don't worry if you are having trouble. The important point here is that dislocations are the primary mechanism of permanently moving planes of atoms relative to one another. Without dislocations, most of our metallic systems would be brittle and behave like ceramics.

Interfacial Defects

We will focus primarily on grain boundaries but for completeness here are a list of various interfacial defects.

  • Grain Boundaries: Interfaces between crystals with different orientations. Can be characterized by the degree of misorientation (rotation) between the grains and are often referred to as high-angle or low-angle grain boundaries.
  • Phase Boundaries: Separate different phases within a material. We will hold off on discussing these until module 9 on phase diagrams.
  • Stacking Faults: Errors in the stacking sequence of atomic planes. Imagine the ABCABC... packing of fcc (111) planes. A stacking fault would be a sequence where we skip one of the "C" positions. For example, ABCABABCABC..., here the stacking fault is between the "BA" planes shown in bold.
  • Surface: The surface is an interfacial defect as the atoms on the surface are missing neighbors.

Concept Check

Answer the questions below, then click on the card to reveal the correct answer.

↺

How do dislocations affect the mechanical properties of materials?

Dislocations allow plastic deformation to occur more easily, making materials more ductile but reducing their yield strength.

↺

What is the difference between a substitutional and an interstitial impurity?

A substitutional impurity replaces a host atom in the lattice, while an interstitial impurity fits into the spaces between the host atoms.

↺

What is a grain boundary, and why does it form?

A grain boundary is the interface between different crystal grains in a polycrystalline material, forming due to variations in crystallographic orientation during solidification.

Conclusion

Defects in materials even at the atomic scale are essential for manipulating and improving the properties of materials.
